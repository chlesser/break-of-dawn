## Turns, Operations, and Combat Time
When considering a battle between a Spartan Fireteam and their foes, it is crucial to understand what is happening and when. What may be several minutes of planning and discussion to the players may be but a split-second to the Spartans they control. As such, Combat in *Break of Dawn* is divided into *Rounds*, which are themselves composed of a number of *Turns*, which are themselves further subdivided into units called *Operations*.

Each *round* of combat represents 6 seconds of real time. In-game, a round of combat lasts until all creatures involved in that combat have taken a *turn*. A new round begins from the top of the initiative order after the last creature has taken their turn. On an individual turn, a creature takes a number of *Operations*, representing the different actions and feats of derring-do they can perform on their turn. On a single turn, a creature may take one *Primary* Operation, one *Secondary* Operation, and one *Tertiary* Operation, each one representing a split-second action or reaction undertaken in the heat of battle.

The order in which creatures’ turns progress in a given round of combat is determined at the very beginning of the combat encounter by rolling for *Initiative*.

### Initiative  
At the beginning of combat, all creatures involved in the combat will roll a d100 adding their dodge bonus. The creature with the highest total takes the first turn in each round of combat, followed by the second highest, third highest, and so on until all creatures have taken a turn and the next round of combat, where the turn order begins again from the creature with the highest total. If two or more creatures tie when rolling for initiative, the creature with the higher base roll, excluding bonuses, goes first. If there is still a tie, the creatures must each roll another d100 without any bonuses to determine who goes first, with the highest roll winning the tie.

### Operations
#### Primary Operations
Primary Operations are the main actions you do on your turn. During your turn in combat, there are many different options that you can choose. The Operations you can take are:

**Attack:**   
Use your Primary Operation to make an attack against a creature within range of the weapon. You can attack up to as many times as your current weapon allows.

**Use an Item:**  
Some items require a Primary Operation to use. You can expend a Primary Operation to immediately gain the effect of one such item that you possess. 

**Revive:**  
Use your Primary Operation to stabilize an unconscious ally; they regain 10 health and are exposed to the Wounded effect.

**Grenade:**  
Use your Primary Operation to make an attack with a grenade at a specified target. You may only do this if you have access to grenades. 

**Punch:**   
As a Primary Operation, a creature may make a punch attack against a target within 5 feet. The target must succeed on a dodge DC 65 or take 21 (6d6) kinetic damage. If the target succeeds the dodge, they take no damage.

#### Secondary Operations  
Some abilities are triggered in a Secondary Operation. On a creature’s turn, they may take one Secondary Operation before or after other types of Operations. It does not count as your Primary Operation, meaning that a creature can take a Secondary Operation and Primary Operation in the same turn.

**Reload:**  
A creature can use a Secondary Operation to reload their weapon after rolling a 1 on an Ammo Die (explained further in the Weapon Statistics section).

#### Tertiary Operations  
A Tertiary Operation is the smallest Operation a creature can take on its turn. Like a Secondary Operation, a creature may make one Tertiary Operation before or after any other Operations on its turn, and can be used alongside both Primary and Secondary Operations.

**Change Weapons:**   
A creature can use a Tertiary Operation to switch to another weapon in their possession. When they switch to a weapon, they may only make attacks with that weapon until they expend a Tertiary Operation to switch to a new weapon on another turn. 

**Interact:**  
Use a Tertiary Operation to interact with or activate a certain object within your reach, such as a door, console, lever, dropped weapon, etc. 

#### Reactive Operations  
Certain abilities are used as Reactive Operations. These are special Operations that a creature takes as a result of an external trigger or event at no cost to the creature’s other Operations. Depending on the trigger, a creature may be able to use Reactive Operations even when it is not its turn. Abilities that cost Reactive Operations will specify that they cost Reactive Operations as well as their trigger. 

#### Other Operations  
At the GM’s discretion, any other actions not listed above could be designated as certain Operations. 

#### Maneuvers
In addition to the actions described above, there are many [[Combat Maneuvers]] that may not always succeed. These may require multiple operations and/or an interaction check to succeed. They are detailed in the [[Combat Maneuvers]] or [[Combat Maneuvers By Cost]] sections.

### Damage and Injury  
Ultimately, the goal of combat will be either to sufficiently injure your opponent so that they are no longer a threat to you, or to prevent them from doing so to you. By taking the *Attack* Primary Operation, a creature makes an attack with the weapon they are currently armed with against a target creature (or target creatures, dependent on the nature of the attack and the weapon used), who must then attempt to avoid the attack (by dodging) or suffer injury (damage) from it, reducing the target creature’s Health by a certain amount (see **Health**, **Damage Types**, and **Death**, below)

#### Health  
Health represents the amount of physical trauma a creature or vehicle can sustain without dying or being destroyed. When a creature or vehicle takes damage, they lose health. The only ways to recover lost health are through downtime (see **Downtime**) or through using a medpac (see **Medpacs**). When a creature’s health is reduced to 0, they are dead, and when a vehicle’s health is reduced to 0, it is destroyed. However, when a Spartan’s health is reduced to 0, they instead fall unconscious (see **Death**). 

#### Shields  
Many creatures and vehicles possess Shields in addition to their base health. Shields are a semi-tangible energy field that protects a creature from harm. When a creature with shields takes damage from an attack, they take the damage directly to their shields instead of their health, only taking damage to their health if they have 0 shields remaining with the exception of certain damage types (such as Hardlight). The Primary advantage of shields is that they can regenerate at a much faster rate than a creature’s health. When a creature with shields goes one full round of combat without taking any new damage to their shields, they regain up to half of their maximum shields (rounded down) at the start of their turn, even if they have no shields remaining. You only gain the benefits of shields while wearing armor.

#### Medpacs  
All Spartans can carry up to 3 medpacs at any given time and can be used to heal health. The amount healed is equal to half the amount of a Medicinal interaction (rounded down), including all bonuses.

#### Death  
Creatures are killed when they are reduced to 0 health. A Spartan, on the other hand, is merely exposed to the Unconscious effect at 0 health. When a Spartan reaches 0 health, they are exposed to the Unconscious effect until they die, regain health in any way, or any ally uses the Revive Operation. While the Spartan is unconscious, they can continue to lose health, potentially bringing them to a negative health value. When a Spartan’s health reaches a negative amount equal to their maximum base health, they die permanently. In addition, on each of their turns after being exposed to the Unconscious effect, the damage dice for the attack that brought the Spartan to 0 health are rerolled, and they continue to take damage from the attack until the Unconscious effect ends or they die. An ally may use a Primary Operation to revive an unconscious Spartan, giving them 10 health and exposing them to the Wounded effect.

### Dodging   
A Dodge Roll is a roll made to avoid an attack. When a creature is targeted by an attack, they must make a dodge roll by rolling a d100 against the Dodge DC of the weapon the attack was made with. A successful dodge roll means they take no damage, while a failed dodge roll means they suffer the damage dealt by the attack. 

#### 1s and 100s on Dodges  
When a creature rolls a 1 or 100 on a dodge, special circumstances take place. When a creature rolls a 1 on a dodge, they are exposed to the Fallen effect and must spend a Primary Operation to get back up as well as half of their movement speed (rounded down), as stated by the Fallen effect. In the event that a 100 is rolled, they are exposed to the Indomitable effect until the end of their next turn. All creatures are exposed to these effects if they roll a 1 or 100\. These effects do not apply to vehicles.

### Attacks
#### Chance Attack  
A Chance Attack is a special kind of attack. If a creature moves within 15 feet of a hostile creature then that hostile creature may make a single attack against that creature as a Reactive Operation, provided they are not surprised by that creature and are within range of their current weapon. 

#### Calling Shots  
When making an attack with any weapon that does not deal explosive damage, a creature may call their shot prior to making the attack. When doing so, they can use any of their attacks with that weapon to fire at specific parts of the target with a specific penalty. Creatures of different sizes may be more difficult to hit in certain spots, and thus get extra bonuses to their dodge rolls against called shots. Small creatures add \+5 to dodges against called shots, and large creatures subtract \-5. The penalties for different types of called shots as well as the effects the target suffers on a hit are listed below. 

**Legs:**   
Penalty: \-15 Dodge DC  
Effects: On a hit, deal the weapon’s damage and decrease the creature’s speed by \-10 feet, which lasts until the target regains health in any way.

**Arms:**   
Penalty: \-25 Dodge DC  
Effects: On a hit, deal the weapon’s damage and decrease the Dodge DC of the creature’s attacks made with the use of their arms or anything they are holding in their arms by 10\. Additionally, on a hit, roll a d6. On a 1, the creature drops any object they are holding, and must expend half their movement in addition to their Tertiary Operation to pick it up. These effects last until the target regains health in any way.

**Head:**  
Penalty: \-35 Dodge DC  
Effects: On a hit, add an extra amount of damage die equal to the base weapon damage.

#### Precision Weapons  
Certain weapons are designated as Precision weapons. Weapons with this designation are more accurate and more effective at hitting called shots. The bonuses they get to doing so are detailed in the weapon’s statistics.

#### Damage Types  
**Kinetic:** The most basic of damage types. Kinetic damage is dealt by physical blows, such as bullets, blades, fists, and falling.   
**Acid:** Damage taken from exposure to corrosive chemicals or other agents that would cause burning or abrasion, such as being digested by an enormous beast. Creatures who take acid damage with no shields suffer an additional \+2 damage die.   
**Explosive:** Explosive damage is dealt by rockets, grenades and other explosive materials. Explosive damage is unique in that vehicles suffer full damage from explosive attacks (for more information, see Vehicles).   
**Hardlight:** Hardlight is a special kind of damage inflicted by Hard-Light weapons (weapons that fire a projectile composed of solid light, such as the Light Rifle). When Hardlight damage is dealt to a shielded target, one damage die can be inflicted directly to the target’s base health, ignoring shields.   
**Plasma:** Plasma is a special sort of damage dealt primarily by Covenant weaponry. Plasma weapons are extra effective against shields, dealing an extra \+1 damage die to shields but \-1 damage dice to base health. If an attack dealing plasma damage were to reduce a target’s shields to 0, only half of the remaining damage (rounded down) would be dealt to the target’s health.  
**Shock:** Shock damage generally includes damage from electrical sources, such as stun grenades. When exposed to shock damage, in addition to the damage dealt, the target must succeed on a Fortitude interaction, the DC of which is dependent on the source of the damage, or lose their Secondary and Tertiary Operations. On a successful check, the target loses no Operations.  
**Thermal:** Thermal damage refers to damage suffered as a result of extremely high temperatures, such as from fire and flames. When a creature takes thermal damage, they take the same amount of damage at the end of each of their successive turns unless they expend their Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Operations to extinguish themselves, ending the damage. If a creature is affected by multiple sources of Thermal damage, they take the damage from each of the sources at the end of each turn, but end all damage when they decide to extinguish themselves.

#### Attacking Through Allies and Other Creatures  
If an attack passes through a space already occupied by a creature, that creature must make a dodge roll against the attack with an additional \+30 Dodge Bonus to their roll (provided they are aware of the attack) or be hit by the attack instead of the target. If the creature is not aware of the attack, they make the dodge at the normal Dodge DC.

### Special Circumstances in Combat
#### Surprise  
When a creature makes an attack against a target that is unaware of its presence, the target is exposed to the Surprised effect until the start of its next turn, but is made aware of its attacker. Additionally, if it is surprised by all enemy creatures involved in combat, they lose \-20 to their initiative rolls for that instance of combat. 

#### Difficult Terrain  
Some areas may be deemed as difficult terrain by the GM. In these areas, all creatures’ speed is halved (rounded down). Some examples of potentially difficult terrain are waist deep water, some mountainous areas, or other places that could make a creature travel slower. If they are attempting to move through the space of a creature, that is also classified as difficult terrain. Creatures cannot move through the space of any hostile creatures unless that creature has been exposed to the Fallen effect, in which case moving through that creature’s space becomes difficult terrain. Additionally, climbing any objects that the GM determines as climbable counts as difficult terrain unless using *Anti-Gravity Armor* or similar abilities.

#### Picking up Objects  
For a creature to pick up any dropped object, such as a weapon that has been knocked out of their hand or a small item that has been discarded, they must take the Interact Operation, which requires them to spend their Tertiary Operation to pick the item up.

#### Dual Wielding  
Certain weapons can be dual wielded. If a creature has a weapon in each hand, they can choose to make one additional attack as a Secondary Operation using the weapon in their second hand while still making the normal amount of attacks with the weapon in their first hand. A weapon can only be dual wielded if it has the “Dual Wielding” trait listed in its statistics.

#### Nonlethal Damage  
To administer non-lethal damage, you must make a called shot against either the arms or legs when using a weapon. When making a melee attack, you can choose to make the damage nonlethal without having to make any called shots. When a creature is reduced to 0 health with nonlethal damage they are exposed to the Unconscious effect instead of being killed.

#### Cover  
The Game Master might decide that some barriers, such as a wall, are penetrable by bullets or certain weapons. All barriers will fall under 1 of 7 categories of cover. Each level of cover has a threshold of health for each 10 foot segment. When a segment of cover’s health is entirely depleted, it is destroyed, and becomes difficult terrain occupying the same space as where the cover previously stood, ending all benefits to creatures standing behind the segment of cover. 

It is possible for attacks to penetrate cover to hit the creatures behind. A creature attempting to shoot through cover must reduce the damage of their attack by a certain number of dice depending on the level of cover, and the target creature receives a bonus to dodge rolls made against the attacks. In this case, the source of cover takes an amount of damage equal to half of the damage rolled (rounded down) after the damage die reduction. If the damage die reduction exceeds the amount of damage die the weapon deals on a standard attack, then the weapon cannot penetrate the cover or target creatures behind it. Alternatively, a creature may choose to attack the segment of cover directly, dealing full damage to the cover without any dodge roll required.

#### Downtime  
Downtime is any period of time no shorter than 6 hours spent by the Fireteam when they are not in immediate danger (such as combat) and not performing strenuous physical activity, such as intense labor. After a period of downtime, all creatures regain all health and shields previously lost, as well as recharging any abilities with limited usage, such as Active Camo or Armor Lock

#### Zero Gravity  
Some scenarios will have creatures working and fighting in 0 gravity. While in 0 gravity, all creatures’ speeds are halved (rounded down). However they can move vertically as well as horizontally. Fly speeds are unaffected by this penalty. In addition, while in 0 gravity you cannot be exposed to the Fallen effect.

#### Underwater  
When in water too deep for a creature to stand in, they are exposed to the following effects:

- Movement speed is halved  
- \-20 to all dodges  
- The range of your weapon is halved  
- Your melee damage is halved  
- Cannot be exposed to the Fallen effect

A Spartan’s suit retains enough oxygen to survive underwater unassisted for up to 5 minutes.

#### In Darkness  
Some instances in the game may have creatures in dark effects. There are three degrees of darkness with different penalties, there are as follows:

**Dim Light**:   
- \-5 to weapon and melee Dodge DC and Detection, Tracking, and Tactics interactions  
- \+5 to all Stealth interactions  
**Darkness**:   
- -10 to weapon and melee Dodge DC and Detection, Tracking, and Tactics interactions  
- \+10 to all Stealth interactions  
- If you use more than half of your movement on your turn you must make a DC 30 dodge or be exposed to the Fallen effect.  
**Void**:   
- \-15 to weapon/melee Dodge DC and Detection, Tracking, and Tactics interactions  
- \+15 to all Stealth interactions
If you use more than half of your movement in one turn, you must make a DC 40 dodge  
or be exposed to the Fallen effect.

#### Fall Damage  
In some instances, creatures may have the misfortune to fall great distances. The damage taken from falling is equal to 4 (1d6) for every 10 feet fallen, with a cap at 40d6 at a height of 400 feet. If the GM determines a fall to be from an unreasonably high height, such as falling from orbit, they are within their authority to simply declare the falling creature dead on impact with no damage dealt. 

#### Exhaustion  
At the GM’s discretion, creatures may become exposed to exhaustion, representing extreme, sustained physical exertion without rest. When exposed to exhaustion, a creature suffers a \-5 penalty to their movement speed, dodge rolls, and any interactions they make. Exhaustion is cumulative, meaning that a creature already suffering from exhaustion who is exposed to exhaustion again suffers an additional \-5 penalty to the above attributes. If a creature is exposed to enough exhaustion to reduce their speed to 0, they are exposed to the Unconscious effect, and if they suffer any further exhaustion before resting during downtime, they die. All exhaustion levels suffered by a creature are removed after resting during downtime.

#### Gun and Grenade Slots  
Each Spartan has a certain amount of gun slots. Different weapons take up a different amount of slots as determined by their size. The same is true for grenades. Each Spartan has a limited amount of grenade slots and each individual grenade takes up a single grenade slot. If a Spartan attempts to carry more guns or grenades than they have slots for, their speed is halved and they suffer \-20 to all dodges. The amount of gun slots each size of weapon requires is listed below:

**Small:** ½   
**Medium:** 1  
**Large:** 2  
**Massive:** 3
